## The Psychology of Investing

### The "Don't Look" Rule
- Academic paper shows: **the more people look at their investments, the less risk they take and the lower their returns**
- Daily checking = stress = selling when down = bad outcomes
- Long-term investors who "buy and hold" do better than daily watchers

### Why This Matters
- Stock market goes up and down daily
- Makes it *seem* risky
- Reality: stocks are safer than people think for long-term (10+ year) investors

## The 10 Biggest Money Mistakes

### 1. Not Earning Enough Money
- People feel stuck
- **Solution**: Invest in human capital (education, skills, entrepreneurship)
- Acquire rare but complementary skill stacks

> "It's not just the skills you have, it's the market where you sell those skills that wildly changes your earning potential."

**Example**: Biotech writer earned $250K vs $50K writers — same writing skill, different market.

### 2. Not Saving Enough
- Wealth compounds over time
- Starting early matters HUGE
- Hard to catch up if you wait
- "It's like health — wake up at 55 with heart disease, it's very difficult to reverse"

### 3. Not Setting Financial Goals
- Without goals, people chase "more money" without purpose
- Or buy a house because "that's what you're supposed to do"
- **Method**: 
  1. List 4 goals
  2. Double them (8 total)
  3. Map to PERMA model (Positive emotion, Engagement, Relationships, Meaning, Accomplishment)

### 4. Spending on Wrong Things
- $12 coffee every day → $150K+ lost over 40 years (at 7%)
- If it doesn't contribute to your PERMA goals, it's probably not worth it

### 5. Not Taking Investment Risks
- **Huge mistake**: Not participating in stock market
- Opportunity cost: 5% (stocks ~7% - cash ~2%)
- Over 40 years: $10K → $150K at 7%

> "Not participating in the stock market when you could is a huge mistake."

### 6. Taking Wrong Investment Risks
- Picking individual stocks
- Trading options
- Crypto speculation
- High fees erode returns

**Tip**: Just use low-cost index funds.

### 7. Missing Tax Planning Opportunities
- Use accounts like RRSP/TFSA (Canada) or Roth/Traditional IRA (US)
- Get a good CPA/accountant

### 8. Missing Estate Planning
- Write a will, especially if you have dependents
- Without one, the government's default applies

### 9. Who You Marry Matters
- Research: "tight wads" (don't like spending) and "spend thrifts" (love spending) actually attract each other
- Opposites *do* attract initially, but cause more marital conflict over money
- Compatibility matters for long-term financial success

**Quiz**: Do you feel pain when spending? (Carnegie Mellon research)

### 10. Underinsuring Catastrophic Risks
- Term life insurance is cheap
- Disability insurance important if you rely on your income

## Renting vs. Owning: The Real Costs

### Unrecoverable Costs of Home Ownership
| Cost | Description |
|------|--------------|
| Mortgage interest | Gone forever |
| Opportunity cost | Money in house = not in stocks |
| Property taxes | 0.5-1%+ annually |
| Maintenance | **1-2%+** — "most underestimated" |
| Emergency repairs | Big ticket items (roof, foundation) |
| Renovation spending | You always upgrade beyond baseline |

### The 5% Rule
- Add up: 1% property tax + 1% maintenance + 3% opportunity cost = **5%**
- Formula: Price ÷ 5% = breakeven monthly rent
- Example: $300K house → $1,250/month rent = breakeven
- If you can rent for less, rent. If more, buy.

**Ben's story**: Rented with wife and 4 kids for 6 years. Moved 4 times as family grew. Would have paid huge transaction costs to own.

### When to Buy
- Very risk averse
- Want to stay in one place long-term
- Have high tax rates (home sales often tax-favored)

### When NOT to Buy
- Young and mobile
- Uncertain about location
- Can't afford maintenance reserves
- Expecting stock-like returns (historical, not guaranteed)

### The Happiness Question
- Controlled studies: **No happiness difference** between renters and owners in similar neighborhoods
- Uncontrolled: renters in worse neighborhoods are less happy (but that's the neighborhood, not renting)

> "If you really want to stay in one place, owning is the way. But it's a double-edged sword — if you realize you want to leave, you might be stuck."

## Investment Strategy: What Actually Works

### The Simple Answer
- Use low-cost index funds
- Capture market returns
- Don't try to beat the market

### The Controversial Paper
Ben referenced a study analyzing 39 countries, 1890-2010, simulating a million lifetimes:

**Finding**: 100% equity (stocks) portfolio is optimal

- **1/3 domestic stocks** (your home country)
- **2/3 international stocks**

Why? 
- Diversifies against domestic problems (inflation, recession)
- Bonds get crushed during high inflation
- The "age-based bond allocation" conventional wisdom is wrong

> "Stocks are a little bit safer for long-term investors than we thought, and bonds are a little bit riskier."

### Lump Sum vs Dollar Cost Averaging
- **Lump sum wins** statistically (more time in market)
- But only invest if you won't panic-sell during downturns

### What to Avoid
- **Covered call ETFs**: You give up too much upside for small income
- **Thematic ETFs**: Created at market peaks (AI, crypto, cannabis, clean energy) — poor long-term returns
- **High-fee products**: They compound against you
- **Crypto**: No fundamental value, pure speculation

### The Efficient Market Concept
- Prices reflect all known information
- Hard to beat the market — even professionals mostly don't
- Professional managers who do beat the market rarely continue to do so

## The Advice for Young People

### Age 20s: Don't Feel Pressured to Save
- Research shows saving when you're young (low income) may be suboptimal
- Instead: Invest in skills, knowledge, network
- Human capital is your biggest asset early on

### The Key Skills
- **Knowledge + Skills** = rare and complementary stack
- Combine with other rare skills (like content creation) for huge upside

### Timeline
- If you have 20+ years: 100% stocks
- If you need money in <5 years: bonds/cash

## AI and Your Career

### Will AI Take Your Job?
- Similar to ATM/bank teller story: ATMs → *more* bank tellers (more branches, lower costs)
- Jevons Paradox: When something becomes more efficient, usage increases, not decreases
- New jobs created we can't foresee

### What Should You Do?
- Acquire rare, complementary skills
- Don't rely on one narrow skillset
- AI will change work, but humans will still be needed
